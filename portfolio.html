session 1 - getting started

The first assignment was to design and make sketches of tinkering "building blocks" – which, despite the name, don't technically have to be blocks). I started by writing down some tinkering resources I already knew, followed by the reason why each one what made those such great building blocks. I thought that by writing down what made other resources great, I would be inspired to make something great as well! (It's the idea that counts...)

The tinkering resources included LEGO, Knex, Temsi, and magnetic sticks and balls. Having written down for every single one why those were great examples of tinkering building blocks, I then felt like LEGO deserved a bit more, which is why I then filled another page just dedicated to LEGO.



The great thing about LEGO is that it is an entire system consisting of multiple techniques. There is the brick-based LEGO, which is not only simple to build with, but also enables you to make something that properly represents whatever you are building. A LEGO car looks like an actual car, a LEGO house looks like an actual house etc. There is also the technical side to LEGO, which makes use of sticks and is more suited for mechanical builds. LEGO Technic makes use of this type.

Thus, LEGO can be used for pretty much anything tinkering related. So now the question was: what can I come up with?

I sketched a bunch of concepts, all displayed underneath. Next to these sketches I added in keywords the core idea and the type of tinkering resource this concept was built upon.


Developing these sketches made me realise something: if you come up with something you think is an original idea, LEGO has already made it...

So while coming up with ideas was difficult for me, I did like the ideas of 3 and 5: Sketch 5 was based on a system of tubes and connection cylinders, while sketch 3 was based on using pencils. I thought: everyone has pencils, why not use them for something else than just drawing?

So I imagined a system that uses pencils as sticks and has little connection-parts that they can snap or be plugged into. These connection parts could have all sorts of shapes, allowing for the connection of pencils at all sorts of angles. This system would then allow for the creation of all sorts of geometric structures.


session 2 - hands on

After making a bunch of sketches last week, this week I started on building one of those building blocks. I decided to work out the pencil connection part tube thingie, so I opened up Blender, made a cylinder, gave it the right dimensions for a pencil, imported it into Cura and let my 3D printer make a rough prototype. As expected, the first tube did not fit, so I adjusted the model and printed it again. This tube was too big and the pencil would not stay in place. After some tweaking I got the right dimensions. Since printing one tube only took around 10 minutes, I let it print a bunch of them.
Of course, the first thing I did when I had a lot of tubes was to connect all my pencils and make one long pencil wand stick with it.



The errors in dimensions did raise the following idea: what if I made tubes of multiple sizes? One tube (colored red for instance) would fit the pencils so that they stay in place, and another tube (colored blue for instance) would be slightly bigger so that the tubes can rotate and is not fixed.



However, before I started working on that idea, I wanted to properly develop the cornerpieces. I first made a simple 90 degrees cornerpiece, but the thought: this should actually be an X-shaped cornerpiece. And if an X-shaped cornerpiece was not enough, I thought to myself: why don’t I develop a cornerpiece with sticks pointing in both the x, y and z directions. So I did. And granted, the first one was too big and I accidentally snapped one piece when trying ot remove its support, but that was nothing that a bit of glue and filing couldn’t solve.



After letting my 3D printer work for a full afternoon making these pieces, I ended up with a whole bunch of parts and allowed myself a fun session of playing with these parts. I managed to make a little catapult, which was probably the highlight of my day.



session 2 - materials table

Tool Affordance Ease of Use Feedback Price/Preciousness Self-guiding Repurposing/Versatility
Story Cubes Many people will understand they have to roll the dice. However, since there are images on there, people might first have to learn the goal of it, which is to create a story starting with "Once upon a time…" and then include all the images on the dice While throwing the dice is quite easy, it is coming up with a story (that includes all/as many) images which might be hard. You can see what you threw right away, but the feedback you get on your story might be dependent on your audience The price kind of depends on where you buy them, but I think they are around 9 euros. So you don't really want to go ahead and change or affect them. Rather high guidance: you just pick them up and roll them. You can reuse the dice as much as you want and create all sorts of combinations, so it is rather versatile.
Arduino Grove Sensor Kit When we opened the box, there were all sorts of cables and parts, but we were not quite clear what we had to do or could do with it. We were all students who had some experience with the normal Arduino, but this just confused us… This kit was not that easy to use, and we were quite overwhelmed when we opened it. Arduino on its own takes some time to understand what to do with it, but this kit made it even more difficult to understand what you had to do with it right away. It took a long while before we got a form of feedback (a blinking LED), and we were not even sure how we got there… The thing is that you don't immediately get feedback when you try something; you have to rerun the program and see if something has changed. This kit is quite expensive. The nice part about Arduino is that the LED's, sensors and servo's are mostly quite cheap. However, these parts are all modified in this kit, so you feel like you have to be extra careful with them. I felt like this kit did not do a proper job in guiding the user to use all the parts. You are a bit limited by what parts you have in the kit.
Arduino Starter Kit The first time I opened my Arduino Kit, I had no idea what to do, but it made sense that the pins had to be placed in the Arduino holes and that you could connect some parts to it that way. I wouldn't say Arduino is that easy to use, but it is not the most difficult. The overall principle of connecting parts, coding and seeing if it works is clear, but every part has their own individual requirements and you need to code it individually. Because you have to code everything, it takes a while to get feedback. While the Arduino itself is quite expensive, most sensors and LED's and servo's are quite cheap. However, I would still not go ahead and simply break them. You could say that the pins and holes in the Arduino and breadboard guide you, but there is not much other guidance in the Arduino Starter Kit than that. I think the Arduino can be combined with many other things, and used for all sorts of purposes, which is nice!
BYOR Thanks to the plug-and-play system, you quite quickly understand how to connect the different parts and can get to work with making all sorts of cool stuff. The parts were rather quickly understood what they were, and they were very quickly connected. In no-time we made a chain reaction of all the sensors and actuators. Because of the plug-and-play, you get immediate feedback when you plug something in. This kit is quite expensive. So I would say you don't want to go and break or adjust things. The plugs and sockets guide you through how to connect everything, and the immediate feedback without the need for coding makes sure you understand what a component does when you plug it in. You are a bit limited by what parts you have in the kit and by the fact that you cannot reprogram some parts.
Makedo When seeing the screws, you can guess what they are supposed to be used for. This is the same for the screwdriver and all the other tools (such as the saw). The idea is rather simple, but we struggled every now and then with getting the screws in. You get feedback right away, especially when your entire build falls apart… Cardboard itself is very cheap, but the kit costs some money. So I would definitely like to get the screws back after making something. The screws and the screwdriver/holepuncher easily guide you. It only really works with cardboard, so you are limited to that.
LEGO Many people will know LEGO, but even if people didn't they would definitely understand how the bricks work because of the knobs and the shape of the bricks. So a rather high affordance. Playing with LEGO is not hard: you can easily click the blocks together. It might get difficult when you want to make all sorts of complicated installations, but the building block itself is still easy to use. You can build stuff pretty quickly with LEGO, so you get feedback right away. Of course, it will take longer if you make gigantic projects. LEGO is expensive… I would almost consider it illegal if someone were to saw, glue or do any other gruesome action with those poor LEGO bricks… The knobs make for LEGO bricks to quite easily be snapped on each other, which guides the user rather well. You can make all sorts of things with LEGO bricks. However, you are limited to the construction-type usage.

session 3 - coding metaphor

For this week’s exercise, we had to come up with a metaphor for coding. Since it has been a while since I have programmed something (and I have forgotten most of my coding-skills), I decided to stick to the primitive coding principles. The first idea I had was about how a program such as in Processing simply constantly loops certain functions. Since I mainly remember loops and if-statements, I decided to think of a metaphor that implements those two ideas. Thus, I came up with a coding game metaphor.

The inspiration behind this was a game we play at scouting, called Mijnenveld (or Landmines). The objective is to get across a chess-like raster field. However, there are landmines scattered across the field, of which the players do now know the location. The player can thus take a step (forward, left, right or backwards) and, dependent on whether there was a landmine there, they can stay on that tile or explode. If they explode, they get back in line and the next player is allowed to go. The players are supposed to memorise where the landmines are placed and what the safe path is to get across.



My game was based on such a thing: in a chess-like field made of tiles, the player has to get across. But there are obstacles in the field! So the player has to program a certain code to get across that the avatar will follow.

In the field there might for instance be a bush. If the player encounters a bush, they have to go around it. So the player can use an if-statement to check if there is a bush, and if so turn 90 degrees.


People can very easily make their own things, and follow the code for step by step (for instance by pointing a pencil at what step they are now). This also teaches them the principle that a computer program simply does what you tell it to, and does not think for itself.
